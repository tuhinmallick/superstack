SmolLM: Tiny Models Trained on High-quality Datasets

Hugging Face fully described their work on SmolLM in this blog post:

SmolLM - blazingly fast and remarkably powerful

Training Data

When training tiny LLMs, we typically need much less training data. It means that we can (almost) exclusively use curated datasets of very high quality and with a high diversity.

Hugging Face used three datasets to train SmolLM:

  • Cosmopedia v2: 28B tokens of synthetic stories and code generated by Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct-v0.1. It covers over 4,000 topics.

  • FineWeb-Edu: A very high-quality dataset of educational content. Hugging Face has demonstrated that using this dataset can train better LLMs than datasets 10 times larger. They use a deduplicated version of FineWeb-Edu, yielding 220B tokens.

  • Python-Edu: 4B tokens of Python code. To create this dataset, they applied the same methodology that they used to create FineWeb-Edu, i.e., using an LLM to annotate and classify samples to keep only the highest quality samples.

In total, there are 252B training tokens. These three datasets are distributed in the SmolLM corpus (odc-by license):

  • HuggingFaceTB/smollm-corpus

Training Architecture and Hyperparameters

SmolLM 135M and 360M were trained on 600B tokens while the 1.7B version was trained on 1T tokens. Since the training dataset has 252B tokens, it means that SmolLM 135M and 360M have been trained for 2.38 epochs (600/252), while the 1.7B version has been trained for 3.96 epochs (1000/252).

As for the neural architecture, they seem to have followed the recommendations provided by Meta in their work on MobileLLM. They use a large number of layers with small embeddings and hidden dimensions, i.e., deep but narrow models:

To give you a point of comparison, Llama 3 8B, which is 60x larger than SmolLM 135M, has only two layers more than SmolLM 135M.

All three models support a small context size of 2048 tokens. They use a tokenizer with a vocabulary of 49152 entries. That’s an extremely large vocabulary for such tiny models and probably suboptimal (i.e., it should be way smaller) as demonstrated by this recent work:

Scaling Laws with Vocabulary: Larger Models Deserve Larger Vocabularies

Hugging Face also published the learning curves:

They show that when they stopped the training, the models were still learning. Continuing pre-training will improve the results. Note: The evaluation plot with Winogrande is particularly interesting. It shows a very unstable accuracy from one checkpoint to another. If you want to draw reliable conclusions on the performance of your LLMs, don’t evaluate on Winogrande alone.

Hugging Face validated the training with these curves above using the same benchmarks they used to evaluate and compare SmolLLM with other models. Don’t do that. If, during training, you look at the performance of the model on the evaluation data, you bias your training to be particularly good on this evaluation data. It becomes much less informative and may lead to overfitting this particular evaluation benchmark. Good scientific practices in machine learning require separate evaluation datasets never seen during training (not by the model, but also not by yourself). To be fair, Hugging Face is not the only organization to do this.

Supervised Full Fine-tuning for SmolLM 135M and 360M

Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) is performed to teach the LLM how to answer human prompts. The prompts can also be completed by system instructions to constrain the model’s style and behavior, for instance, to make the LLM safer.

There are many instruction datasets freely available on the Hugging Face Hub. However, almost none of them tackles a particular domain or style. Moreover, they are almost all in English.

If you want to cover a particular language or domain for your applications, you will have to generate an instruction dataset by yourself. This can be easily achieved with instruct LLMs, for instance using Microsoft’s instruction synthesizer (article from The Salt):

I particularly recommend this approach to create data for fine-tuning tiny LLMs. Small models learn faster and better from synthetic data (it’s a kind of knowledge distillation).

For this article, I used HuggingFaceH4/ultrachat_200k, a multi-turn instruction dataset generated by other LLMs that doesn’t target a particular domain. We will only use the column “messages” which is automatically processed by Hugging Face’s TRL for fine-tuning. Note: If you prepare your own dataset, you can use the same JSON format.

The dataset is large and with long sequences of tokens. For this tutorial, I only ran 4,000 steps, with a batch size of 16 and a sequence length of 2048. The full fine-tuning code is in the notebook. It’s very standard except that, in contrast with most of my other tutorials, I didn’t use LoRA or quantization since the models are small enough to be fully fine-tuned on consumer hardware.

The learning curves:

As you can see, the model was still learning (loss still decreasing). I recommend training the model for at least one epoch for better results.

Distilled DPO

With DPO, we can easily, and efficiently, align the model with human preferences. Here, we will do “distilled” DPO. It’s “distilled”, as in “knowledge distillation”, because the dataset we are going to use for this training has been fully generated by other LLMs.

DPO requires a dataset comparing two different LLM outputs. One of the outputs is “chosen” while the other one is “rejected”. With DPO, we want the LLM to learn to generate “chosen” outputs while being more distant from the “rejected” output. In the case of distilled DPO, an LLM has judged which one of the outputs is the chosen one, while in regular DPO, a human would have made this choice to create the dataset.

I used HuggingFaceH4/ultrafeedback_binarized for this training. It contains 61.1k training examples of pairs of chosen-rejected outputs for a given prompt. Note: I use the “*_prefs” splits.

For training, I’ll use TRL’s DPOTrainer but this dataset is not in the format expected by this trainer. We need to apply a chat template to the “chosen” and “rejected” columns, as follows.

dataset = load_dataset("HuggingFaceH4/ultrafeedback_binarized", split=["train_prefs","test_prefs"])

def process(row):
    row["chosen"] = tokenizer.apply_chat_template(row["chosen"], tokenize=False)
    row["rejected"] = tokenizer.apply_chat_template(row["rejected"], tokenize=False)
    return row

dataset[0] = dataset[0].map(
    process,
    num_proc= multiprocessing.cpu_count(),
    load_from_cache_file=False,
)

dataset[1] = dataset[1].map(
    process,
    num_proc= multiprocessing.cpu_count(),
    load_from_cache_file=False,
)

Then, the DPO training code is almost the same as for SFT. The main difference is that we use the DPOTrainer instead of the SFTTrainer, with a new hyperparameter, beta, that I set to 0.1 (default value). You can try to find a better beta but 0.1 often yields nearly optimal results.

The full training code is in the notebook.

In particular, for the DPOTrainer:

trainer = DPOTrainer(
    model,
    ref_model=ref_model,
    args=training_arguments,
    train_dataset=dataset[0],
    eval_dataset=dataset[1],
    tokenizer=tokenizer
)

“model” is the base SmolLM initializing DPO training while ref_model is the SFT checkpoint that will be used as a reference model by DPO. You could also use this checkpoint to initialize DPO training. Sometimes, it can accelerate convergence (faster training).

During my experiment, the validation loss decreased, indicating that the models were learning effectively. However, as indicated by the learning curves above, the models still gave higher rewards to the rejected answer while the gap between “chosen” and “rejected” answers remains more or less the same. It means that the models are not learning to prefer the “chosen” answers. My assumption is that they are either too small for this task or require a much higher learning (or a much lower beta).

Conclusion

Fully fine-tuning tiny models of less than 500M parameters is feasible and fast on consumer hardware. However, even for tiny LLMs, we still can’t set large batch sizes and gradient checkpointing remains necessary.

Even after fine-tuning, I found that the models frequently ignore the instructions in the prompt during inference. A behavior that is also obvious in the instruct versions released by Hugging Face.

The training datasets I used weren't focused enough on specific tasks or domains, i.e., the task might been a bit too difficult to learn for these models with only a few thousand training steps. Instead, if you use specialized training data for a single domain, focusing on simple tasks like yes/no answering, classification, or other tasks that don't require generating complex responses, the models are likely to perform very well.
