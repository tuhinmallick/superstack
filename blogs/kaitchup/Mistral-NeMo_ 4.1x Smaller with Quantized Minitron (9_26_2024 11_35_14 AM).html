Mistral-NeMo: 4.1x Smaller with Quantized Minitron

How Pruning, Knowledge Distillation, and 4-Bit Quantization Can Make Advanced AI Models More Accessible and Cost-Effective

NVIDIA's Minitron compresses large language models (LLMs) by pruning the least important weights, followed by retraining through knowledge distillation. This approach significantly reduces model sizes while preserving their accuracy.

NVIDIA released Minitron versions of Llama 3.1 and Mistral-NeMo, reducing their number of parameters from 8B to 4B and 12B to 8B, respectively.

Why is this important?

While Mistral-NeMo cant run on a consumer GPU, its Minitron version can. A 24 GB GPU would be enough. However, this could also be achieved by quantizing Mistral-NeMo. 4-bit quantization methods are now accurate enough.

But what if we could also quantize a Minitron model? Is quantization still accurate enough for a model that has been pruned with Minitron?

For instance, a 4-bit version of Mistral-NeMo-Minitron would run on an 8 GB GPU, significantly bringing down inference costs.

In this article, I review the Minitron approach, exploring how to compress LLMs through pruning and knowledge distillation. We will then discuss quantizing these Minitron models to 4-bit precision using AutoRound. The last section presents the evaluation results.

The findings indicate that Minitron models are strong candidates for 4-bit quantization, with only minimal accuracy loss. Notably, the 4-bit Mistral-NeMo-Minitron outperforms Llama 3.1 8B while using 10.1 GB less memory, making it capable of running on a 12 GB GPU, or an 8 GB GPU for short sequences or with a quantized KV cache.

Compressing LLMs with Minitron: Teacher Correction, Pruning, and Knowledge Distillation

The Minitron approach was first described in this paper:

Compact Language Models via Pruning and Knowledge Distillation

And then NVIDIA further improved Minitron to apply it to LLMs whose pre-training data are unknown:

LLM Pruning and Distillation in Practice: The Minitron Approach

Note: The second paper is much easier to read if you dont have a research background in AI. It also describes very well the mechanics behind Minitron.

Minitron, as applied to Mistral NeMo and Llama 3.1, is a 3-step approach:

  1. Continue pre-training the teacher (e.g., Llama 3.1 8B) on the same dataset that will be used for distillation.

  2. Prune the teachers weights.

  3. Fine-tune the pruned model (Student) on the full dataset using the teacher for the knowledge distillation.

The teacher of Llama-3.1-Minitron-4B is Llama 3.1 8B and the teacher of Mistral-NeMo-Minitron-8B is Mistral NeMo 12B.

NVIDIA found that step 1 is critical. Using Mistral NeMo 12B directly as a teacher is suboptimal due to differences in sub-word token distributions between the original dataset (on which the model was pre-trained) and the dataset to be used for distillation. For this training, they used 127 billion tokens. Note: This training doesnt improve the performance of the teacher. Its only useful for the distillation step.

For step 2, the models are pruned width-wise rather than depth-wise. In other words, the Minitron models have the same number of layers as their teachers but the hidden dimension of the model is reduced. Note: They also released a Minitron version of Llama 3.1 with half the number of layers. This model is faster for inference but does not perform as well as the Minitron version with a smaller hidden dimension.

The weights to be pruned are found with an importance estimation. They employ an activation-based strategy to estimate the importance of each head, neuron, and embedding channel by analyzing the activations from the multi-head attention (MHA), multilayer perceptron (MLP), and LayerNorm layers. This is done using a small calibration dataset of 1,024 samples. This step doesnt appear costly. I think it could be achieved on a consumer GPU for Llama 3.1 8B.

For step 3, NVIDIA chose to use logit-only distillation by minimizing the forward KL divergence loss between the teacher and student model probabilities. This is a costly step. They did it on 32 NVIDIA DGX H100 nodes but I couldnt find for how long.

They used 94B tokens to train Llama 3.1 Minitron 4B and 380B tokens for Mistral Nemo Minitron 8B. Both training used large batches. You cant do this locally, yet.

The resulting models perform very well compared to their teacher:

According to NVIDIA's benchmarks, the Minitron version of Llama 3.1 8B underperforms its teacher model, Llama 3.1 8B, but still surpasses other models of similar sizes. Meanwhile, the Minitron version of Mistral NeMo closely matches the performance of the original Mistral NeMo and even outperforms it on certain tasks. Additionally, it significantly outperforms Llama 3.1 8B, which is of comparable size.

These results suggest that the Minitron approach is effective: it reduces model size while maintaining accuracy. However, an important question remains: Could we achieve similar or even better results by using quantization, which might offer a more cost-effective solution than Minitron?

For example, intuitively, quantizing Mistral NeMo to 4-bit using a state-of-the-art method like AutoRound could produce a model similar in size to its Minitron version, with comparable performance.

Of course, we could also quantize the Minitron models to further reduce their size. However, quantization of pruned models is largely understudied, and since quantization tends to introduce more inaccuracies in smaller models, this could potentially degrade their performance.

Lets find out!

Quantizing Minitron Models with AutoRound

To quantize LLMs, Im currently using a lot AutoRound. This is a state-of-the-art quantization method proposed by Intel. It performs very well for 4-bit quantization and, while the default hyperparameters perform well for most models, we can adjust them to create better quantized models. I explained the method here:

Intel AutoRound: Accurate Low-bit Quantization for LLMs

Jun 27
Read full story

I used it to create accurate and fast 4-bit versions of Llama 3.1 and Phi-3.5.

To quantize, run, and evaluate, the Minitron models with AutoRound, we need to install the following libraries:

pip install --upgrade transformers auto-round flash_attn optimum auto-gptq

Note: As Im writing this article, the Minitron models are not supported by the latest stable version of Transformers, we need to install it from source:

pip install git+https://github.com/huggingface/transformers

Then, quantization can simply be achieved with this code:

from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM, AutoTokenizer
import torch
model_name = "nvidia/Mistral-NeMo-Minitron-8B-Base"
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(model_name, torch_dtype=torch.float16, trust_remote_code=True)
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(model_name)

from auto_round import AutoRound

bits, group_size, sym = 4, 128, True
autoround = AutoRound(model, tokenizer, bits=bits, group_size=group_size, batch_size=2, seqlen=512, sym=sym, gradient_accumulate_steps=4, device='cuda')
autoround.quantize()
output_dir = "./AutoRound/Mistral-NeMo-Minitron-8B-Base-AutoRound-GPTQ-sym-4bit/"
autoround.save_quantized(output_dir)

The most important hyperparameters are bits, group_size, and sym. I only evaluate 4-bit quantization (bits=4) in this article. 2-bit is also possible but might yield poorly accurate models. You can decrease groupe_size to 64 for more accurate quantization but the model will be slightly larger.

As for symmetric or asymmetric quantization, I usually obtain results good enough with a symmetric quantization. It yields faster models compatible with Marlin. However, it might not perform very well all the time. As we will see in the evaluation section, this is the case for Mistral-NeMo-Minitron-8B-Base for which using asymmetric quantization looks necessary.

I produced two quantized versions, a symmetric version and an asymmetric version, for each Minitron model and their teacher (link in the introduction).

Evaluation of the Quantized Minitron

First, let's examine the model sizes.

How much did the original model shrink after pruning, and how much further reduction was achieved through quantization?

Some observations:

  • A 4-bit model is not 4x times smaller than a 16-bit model, mainly because the embedding parameters are not quantized.

  • AutoRound is slightly less effective than bitsandbytes in reducing the model size, except for the Llama 3.1 Minitron pruned depth-wise.

  • Quantized versions of the teacher models are smaller than their Minitron 16-bit versions.

  • Mistral NeMo 12B occupies 24.5 GB while its Minitron version occupies 16.8 GB. If quantized to 4-bit with AutoRound, it then only occupies 5.96 GB. Thats 24% of the original size of Mistral NeMo!

For the evaluation, I picked three benchmarks: MMLU, MMLU-PRO, and Arc Challenge. I ran them for all the models through the Evaluation Harness with this command:

lm_eval --model hf --model_args pretrained={model_name} --tasks mmlu,arc_challenge,leaderboard_mmlu_pro --device cuda:0 --num_fewshot 0 --batch_size 4 --output_path ./eval/

Note: The tasks discussed here are typically evaluated using a 5-shot (MMLU, MMLU-PRO) or 25-shot (Arc Challenge) approach, where 5 or 25 examples are included in the prompt to guide the model. However, I believe that modern LLMs are sophisticated enough for 0-shot evaluation, meaning they should, like humans, recognize and respond to questions without needing examples. Additionally, including examples in prompts can complicate the reproducibility of evaluations (which examples should be chosen and why?) and significantly increase evaluation costs due to the longer context consuming more memory.

To assess the effectiveness of quantization with AutoRound, we should establish a baseline. For this, I selected bitsandbytes 8-bit and 4-bit quantizations. Implementing bitsandbytes quantization is straightforward. Simply run lm-eval with load_in_8bit=True for 8-bit quantization and load_in_4bit=True for 4-bit quantization, as demonstrated below:

lm_eval --model hf --model_args pretrained={model_name},load_in_8bit=True --tasks mmlu,arc_challenge,leaderboard_mmlu_pro --device cuda:0 --num_fewshot 0 --batch_size 4 --output_path ./eval/

The evaluation results:

Note: Asym is the asymmetric quantization and Sym is the symmetric quantization.

  • The Minitron models are very robust to 4-bit quantization. With AutoRound, the degradation of the performance on these benchmarks is negligible on Arc Challenge and MMLU. Its more obvious on MMLU-PRO.

  • Mistral NeMo Minitron 4-bit outperforms Llama 3.1 8B on Arc Challenge (+3.92 points) and MMLU (+1.09 points) while being 10.1 GB smaller!

  • Mistral NeMo Minitron quantized to 8-bit with bitsandbytes yields almost exactly the same results as Mistral NeMo Minitron not quantized: same results on Arc Challenge, -0.05 points on MMLU-PRO, and -0.01 points on MMLU.

  • AutoRound is more accurate than bitsandbytes. I assume that better results could be achieved with longer optimization and longer sequences, i.e., with more budget.

  • Asymmetric quantization significantly outperforms symmetric quantization. The symmetric quantization of Mistral NeMo Minitron poorly performs. I ran it three times for confirmation and got very unstable results. Ill need some time to identify the reason.

Conclusion

NVIDIA's Minitron compresses large language models (LLMs) by pruning the least important weights and retraining through knowledge distillation, effectively reducing model sizes while preserving accuracy. This approach was successfully applied to models like Llama 3.1 and Mistral-NeMo.

The evaluation showed that Minitron models are strong candidates for 4-bit quantization, with only minimal accuracy loss. Remarkably, the 4-bit Mistral-NeMo-Minitron outperformed Llama 3.1 8B while using significantly less memory, demonstrating the potential of combining Minitron compression with advanced quantization techniques.

The results suggest that while Minitron is an effective approach for compressing LLMs, combining it with quantization can further enhance the efficiency and applicability of these models, making them more accessible without compromising performance.

You can also fine-tune the quantized Minitron with a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method like QLoRA. We saw that it can work very well for models quantized with AutoRound.
