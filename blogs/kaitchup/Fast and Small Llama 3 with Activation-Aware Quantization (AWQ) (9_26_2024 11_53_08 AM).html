Most state-of-the-art large language models (LLMs) are too large to be loaded on a consumer GPU. LLMs with more than 12 billion fp16 parameters can’t be loaded on a high-end GPU with 24 GB of VRAM.

One way to reduce the size of an LLM is quantization. This is an active research area with two very popular algorithms proposed in 2023: GPTQ and bitsandbytes nf4. They are both effective at reducing the size of LLMs while preserving most of their performance in downstream tasks.

Yet, they have also well-identified flaws. For instance, they naively consider all the parameters with the same importance. bitsandbytes nf4 quantized models are also slow during inference.

Activation-Aware Quantization (AWQ) proposed solutions for these issues. AWQ protects important weights and exploits a reorder-free online dequantization to speed up inference.

Introduction to Activation-Aware Quantization

AWQ was proposed by Lin et al. (June 2023):

AWQ: Activation-aware Weight Quantization for LLM Compression and Acceleration (Lin et al., 2023)

Their work is mainly motivated by their observations reported in Table 1 of the paper.

It shows that preserving 0.1% to 1% of the parameters from quantization, based on their activation, can drastically reduce the performance loss. For instance, according to this table, OPT-1.3B achieves a perplexity of 16.4 on Wikitext but once quantized with RTN 3-bit, OPT-1.3B becomes unusable with a perplexity of 206.5. In contrast, preserving 1% of the weights during quantization only increases the perplexity by 2.1. Note: Lower perplexity is better.

It works if we preserve the parameters according to the activation distribution, but preserving them according to their value (“based on W” in the table) doesn’t have much effect.

The problem is that mixing quantization precisions, i.e., keeping FP16 parameters along INT4 or INT3 parameters is hardware inefficient. To avoid mixed precision, they designed a per-channel scaling method to search for the optimal scaling factors that minimize the quantization error for salient parameters. It means that AWQ quantizes all the parameters but scales some of them before quantization.

AWQ also comes with optimized GPU kernels that were developed thanks to the “hardware-friendly nature of AWQ”. Unlike GPTQ, which formulates linear layers as matrix-vector (MV) products, AWQ models these layers as matrix-matrix (MM) multiplications. MV can only be executed on slow CUDA cores while MM can be executed on the faster tensor cores which are in all recent NVIDIA GPUs (at least from the RTX 2xxx generation but they are much faster from the RTX 3xxx generation).

AWQ is much faster than GPTQ quantized models during decoding. We will verify it in the next sections of this article.

Let’s have a look at some of the results of AWQ, considering GPTQ as a baseline.

The authors in their analysis of the results disregarded what they call “GPTQ-R” (row in grey). GPTQ-R is a popular variant of GPTQ (this corresponds to setting “desc_act=True” in the library auto-gptq). It’s slower (-25% to -50% speed) but if we use GPTQ without reordering the performance of the model degrades to a point where it may become worse than the much more naive RTN quantization.

In my opinion, comparing AWQ with GPTQ-R is fair and relevant.

We can conclude from the results that AWQ performs similarly to GPTQ-R while being much faster.

AutoAWQ: Easily Quantize and Run LLMs with AWQ

AWQ is now well integrated into popular libraries and frameworks. For instance, it’s supported by Hugging Face transformers and vLLM.

To quantize LLMs with AWQ, I use AutoAWQ (MIT license). The main reason is that it’s very simple and works similarly to AutoGPTQ which I have already used in previous articles. It’s built on top of Hugging Face transformers.

AutoAWQ only supports 4-bit quantization for now. For lower precision, have a look at the original implementation of AWQ.

We can install AutoAWQ with pip:

pip install autoawq

Activation-Aware Quantization of Llama 3

Quantization of Llama 3 8B can be done with a GPU with 12 GB of VRAM but you need at least 14 GB of CPU RAM.

I used Google Colab Pro. Using the L4 GPU, quantization takes 15 minutes.

You only need to write a few lines of code to quantize with AWQ:

from awq import AutoAWQForCausalLM
from transformers import AutoTokenizer
model_path = 'meta-llama/Meta-Llama-3-8B'
quant_path = 'Llama-3-8b-awq-4bit'
quant_config = { "zero_point": True, "q_group_size": 128, "w_bit": 4 }

# Load model and tokenizer
model = AutoAWQForCausalLM.from_pretrained(model_path)
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(model_path, use_fast=True)

# Quantize
model.quantize(tokenizer, quant_config=quant_config)

# Save quantized model
model.save_quantized("./"+quant_path, safetensors=True)
tokenizer.save_pretrained("./"+quant_path)

Note: Since we are quantizing Llama 3, you will need to sign the Llama 3 license agreement and be connected to your HF account to run this code.

With “safetensors=True”, we serialize the quantized model with safetensors.

Inference with AWQ LLMs: Perplexity, Memory Consumption, and Inference Speed

Inference with AWQ models is only supported by NVIDIA GPUs with the Ampere architecture or more recent. For instance, it will work if you have a GPU from the RTX 3xxx or RTX 4xxx generations. If you use Google Colab, it will only work with the L4 and A100 of Google Colab Pro (the T4 and V100 are too old).

You can run inference with AWQ models as you would with GPTQ models.

First, we load the model and its tokenizer:

from transformers import AutoTokenizer, AutoModelForCausalLM
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained("kaitchup/Llama-3-8b-awq-4bit", torch_dtype=torch.float16, device_map={"": 0})

Note: AWQ kernels are optimized for inference with float16. Setting torch_dtype=torch.float16 yields faster models.

Then, we can prompt the model and decode its answer as we would with a standard LLM:

my_prompt = "Tell me about gravity" 
model_inputs = tokenizer(my_prompt, return_tensors="pt").to("cuda:0")
output = model.generate(**model_inputs, max_length=1000)[0]
print(tokenizer.decode(output, skip_special_tokens=True))

  • Perplexity: AWQ is slightly better than GPTQ.

  • Storage size: On the hard drive, the model quantized with AWQ is smaller than the one quantized with GPTQ.

  • VRAM usage: This is a strange result. GPTQ and AWQ models should approximately consume the same amount of VRAM just after loading. I assume that AutoAWQ consumes extra VRAM to prepare the model for inference just after loading, but I’m not sure.

  • Inference speed: I confirmed that AWQ models are indeed much faster (2.8x).

Conclusion

AWQ is better than GPTQ. It is less naive mainly by preserving salient parameters according to their activation distribution. It results in better and faster models.

AWQ is still less popular than GPTQ. But this might change as AWQ support has been added to popular frameworks serving LLMs such as TGI and vLLM.
